# Punch the Macaque: Growing Up as a Star at Japan's Ichikawa Zoo

In the leafy suburbs of Tokyo, at the modest Ichikawa City Zoo, a 9-month-old Japanese macaque named **Punch** has become an unlikely celebrity. Abandoned by his mother shortly after birth in late 2025, Punch captured global attention by clinging to an orangutan plush toy for comfort, turning him into an internet sensation. Now growing rapidly and ignoring the crowds he attracts, Punch continues to boost visitor numbers at the small zoo, drawing tens of thousands of people eager to see his energetic displays.

## Background: From Abandonment to Internet Fame

Punch's story began in heartbreak. Born at the Ichikawa City Zoo, he was rejected by his mother, a common but tragic occurrence among macaques in captivity. Zookeepers stepped in immediately, providing round-the-clock care. In a touching development, the infant monkey latched onto a stuffed orangutan toy, which became his surrogate companion. Videos of Punch hugging the toy went viral earlier this year, as detailed in a previous report by [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/02/19/world/asia/punch-macaque-monkey-japan.html).

The zoo, home to **56** resident macaques on its Monkey Mountain exhibit, is not a major tourist draw like Tokyo's Ueno Zoo. Before Punch, it saw modest attendance from local families. But his plight resonated worldwide, symbolizing resilience and the bond between animals and their human caregivers. Social media amplified the narrative, with shares and comments flooding platforms. By May 2026, the buzz had translated into real foot traffic, with visitors from as far as Spain making repeat trips to the zoo, located just outside central Tokyo.

This surge highlights how animal stories can spotlight smaller attractions. Ichikawa City, a residential area in Chiba Prefecture, benefits from the proximity—it's a short train ride from Tokyo stations like [Tokyo](https://go2-japan.com/city/tokyo/). Punch's fame underscores Japan's rich wildlife exhibits, where ethical care practices are emphasized, though no specific details on his long-term health are provided beyond his active behavior.

## Punch's Daily Life and Visitor Interactions

At feeding time on Monkey Mountain, the atmosphere is lively. Zookeepers distribute apples, sweet potatoes, and cabbage, prompting grunts and squeals from the troop. Punch, now more independent at 9 months, emerges as the star. He twirls, leaps into the air, hangs upside down while munching a biscuit, and even parades on a zookeeper's arm. In one captured moment, he leaps onto a keeper as food scatters in the enclosure, showcasing his playful agility.

Despite the excitement, Punch largely ignores his admirers. Crowds gather at the blue railing of the enclosure, phones raised to snap photos. One young visitor, **David Lena**, an 11-year-old from Spain, expressed his enthusiasm during a third visit in a week: “Look at him go! I want to hold him so badly!” Such reactions are common, with families and tourists alike drawn to Punch's uninhibited energy.

The zoo's setup allows close but safe viewing, typical of Japanese facilities that prioritize animal welfare. Visuals from the scene show visitors straining for glimpses, underscoring Punch's pull. Reporters Javier C. Hernández and Hisako Ueno observed these interactions firsthand at the zoo, noting how the macaque's antics provide a joyful escape amid everyday routines.

Punch's popularity has revitalized the Ichikawa City Zoo, turning it into a must-see for animal lovers. ## What's Next for Punch and the Zoo

As Punch matures, his role in the troop may evolve. At 9 months, he's transitioning from infant dependency to juvenile exploration, potentially integrating more with other macaques. Zookeepers continue to monitor him closely, but no plans for relocation or special programs are mentioned. His orangutan toy remains a symbol of his early days, though he now engages more with his environment.
