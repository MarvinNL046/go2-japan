### Background on the Postwar Order and Japan's Place in It

The postwar order, a widely recognized framework forged in the aftermath of World War II, has long defined international relations, particularly through alliances anchored by the United States. For **Japan**, this era began with its 1947 constitution, which renounced war and emphasized pacifism, while relying on the US-Japan Security Treaty of 1960 for defense. This treaty, a cornerstone of the alliance, allows US forces to be stationed in Japan, providing a deterrent against regional threats, including from North Korea and historical tensions with China.

Similarly, **Germany** has operated under NATO's protective umbrella since 1955, with its own postwar constitution limiting military engagements to defensive purposes. The Guardian editorial highlights how these arrangements, once seen as unassailable, are now under strain due to perceived unreliability in US commitments. Widely known public discourse points to events like the US withdrawal from the Iran nuclear deal in 2018 and debates over NATO funding as early indicators of alliance fatigue. Although the editorial's full text details specific contemporary triggers—such as evolving US foreign policy under recent administrations—the core theme is the emergence of a "new world" where traditional guarantors of security may no longer hold firm.

Japan's position is particularly delicate. As an island nation with a pacifist stance, it has balanced economic prowess—boasting the world's third-largest economy—with military restraint. Public knowledge underscores Japan's incremental steps toward greater self-reliance, such as the 2015 security legislation allowing collective self-defense. The editorial posits that as US alliances "crumble," Japan and Germany must confront the end of this sheltered era, potentially leading to increased defense spending and closer ties with other partners like the European Union or regional blocs in Asia.

This shift is not without precedent. Historical analyses, part of established public record, note how the Cold War's end in 1991 prompted initial reassessments, but the postwar order persisted until recent global disruptions, including the COVID-19 pandemic and Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, accelerated changes. The Guardian's view frames these nations as harbingers of broader transformation, where economic interdependence clashes with security uncertainties.
