In a surprising ripple effect from the ongoing **Iran war**, Japanese snack powerhouse **Calbee** announced on May 12, 2026, that it would temporarily replace its vibrant, colorful packaging with black-and-white versions for popular products like potato chips. The move, reported from Tokyo, stems from shortages of **naphtha**, a crude-oil derivative essential for producing the inks used in full-color printing. As Japan relies heavily on Middle Eastern imports for over **90 percent** of its oil and **40 percent** of its naphtha, the conflict's disruption to global supply chains has forced this cost-saving and supply-stabilizing measure, according to [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/12/world/asia/calbee-japan-bags-iran-war.html).

## Background on the Supply Chain Disruption

The **Iran war** has intensified since its escalation, leading to widespread havoc in international trade, a sharp spike in oil prices, and bottlenecks in key commodities. Naphtha, derived from crude oil, plays a crucial role not just in inks but also in plastics, making it indispensable for Japan's manufacturing sector. Calbee, one of the country's leading food companies known for its salty snacks, confirmed via a spokeswoman that instability in raw material supplies—specifically naphtha for printing inks—prompted the packaging switch. The company emphasized in its statement that this change aims to "help maintain a stable supply of products" without compromising the quality of the snacks themselves.

Japan's vulnerability is stark: the nation imports the vast majority of its energy needs from the Middle East, leaving it exposed to regional tensions. Recent weeks have seen complaints from various industries beyond food, including manufacturers of cars, bathroom fixtures, and paint, all grappling with naphtha shortages. Food producers have reported challenges in packaging items like tofu and tea leaves, underscoring how even everyday essentials are affected. Calbee's decision, while temporary, symbolizes the broader economic strain on Asia, where countries like Japan are already feeling the war's pinch through higher costs and delayed shipments.

Government response has been measured. **Kei Sato**, a Japanese cabinet official, addressed the issue at a Tokyo news conference on May 12, stating, "We have not received any reports of immediate supply problems for printing inks or naphtha." He added that sufficient quantities appear secured for Japan overall, with ministries actively communicating with affected companies to monitor the situation. This reassurance contrasts with the on-the-ground realities faced by businesses like Calbee, highlighting a potential gap between official optimism and industry challenges.

## Impacts on Japan's Food and Manufacturing Sectors

For Calbee, the shift to monochrome packaging is more than aesthetic—it's a pragmatic response to ensure snacks remain on shelves amid global uncertainty. The company's traditional bags, featuring eye-catching colors and designs, are a staple in convenience stores and supermarkets across Japan, from bustling Tokyo streets to rural vending machines. Black-and-white wrapping may alter consumer perceptions, potentially dampening impulse buys, though Calbee has not speculated on sales impacts.

Wider implications extend to Japan's economy, which is heavily import-dependent. The war's effects on oil and derivatives like naphtha could cascade into higher production costs for plastics and chemicals, affecting everything from bottled beverages to electronics. While the source does not detail long-term forecasts, the International Monetary Fund has previously warned of slowed economic growth due to such conflicts, and central banks are adjusting interest rates in response. In Japan, where food manufacturing is a key industry, these shortages could lead to subtle but noticeable changes in product availability and pricing, though no immediate shortages of the snacks themselves have been reported.
