# Japan's 1950s Reforestation Project Now Causes Mass Hay Fever Crisis

A decision made over 70 years ago to rapidly reforest Japan's war-damaged mountains with just two fast-growing tree species has created an unexpected public health crisis. Today, **43% of Japan's population** experiences medium to severe hay fever symptoms each spring, driven by massive pollen releases from Japanese cedar and cypress plantations—a rate far exceeding that of the UK (26%) and the US (12-18%), according to [BBC reporting](https://www.bbc.com/future/article/20260515-the-1950s-blunder-which-causes-mass-hay-fever-in-japan).

## The Post-War Reforestation Blunder

After World War Two, Japan faced a environmental catastrophe. Wartime fuel shortages had driven widespread deforestation of natural forests, leaving mountains around major cities like Tokyo, Osaka, and Kobe completely stripped bare. These barren landscapes created serious problems: increased landslides, flooding, and soil erosion threatened communities across the archipelago.

To address the crisis quickly, the Japanese government launched a large-scale afforestation program funded by tax revenues. Rather than replanting diverse native species, officials chose an expedient approach: plant only two native, fast-growing evergreen species that could rapidly restore forest cover and provide timber for post-war reconstruction. The chosen trees were the Japanese cedar (_sugi_) and the Japanese cypress (_hinoki_).

The strategy worked—forests were restored quickly. But it created an unintended consequence that would plague the nation for decades: monoculture plantations that produce extraordinary quantities of pollen each spring.

## A National Health Emergency

Today, hay fever—also known as allergic rhinitis—has become a national crisis in Japan. In February, viral videos showed what appeared to be waves of smoke billowing off evergreen forests. The "smoke" was actually pollen, a stark visual warning to tens of millions of residents that allergy season had arrived.

The health and economic impacts are substantial. Beyond the discomfort of sneezing, congestion, and itchy eyes, hay fever sufferers experience sleep loss and poor concentration. The condition also increases the likelihood of developing related conditions such as asthma and food allergies. At peak pollen season, the economic impact from sick days and reduced consumer spending is estimated at **$1.6 billion (£1.2 billion) per day**.

Every spring—now arriving earlier than usual due to climate change—Japanese city streets fill with people wearing masks as protection against pollen. What was once a minor seasonal inconvenience has become a defining feature of the Japanese spring.

## What This Means for Visitors

Travelers planning trips to Japan during spring (typically March through May) should be aware that hay fever season coincides with popular cherry blossom viewing periods. While visitors without pollen allergies can enjoy this time freely, those with allergies may want to consider visiting during other seasons or bringing appropriate medication. Masks remain widely available and socially acceptable to wear in public spaces throughout Japan.

## Looking Forward

The scale of the problem has prompted discussions about potential solutions, though reversing 70 years of monoculture forestry presents significant challenges. Researchers like **Noriko Sato**, a forestry professor at Kyushu University, continue studying the issue and its long-term implications for Japan's environment and public health.

The cedar and cypress plantations that once solved Japan's post-war deforestation crisis now represent a cautionary tale about the unintended consequences of large-scale environmental interventions. What seemed like an efficient solution in the 1950s has become one of modern Japan's most persistent public health challenges.
