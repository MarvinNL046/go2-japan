# 20 Deaths Linked to Amgen Drug in Japan; Prescriptions Halted

Twenty people in Japan have died after taking **Amgen's** rare-disease drug **Tavneos**, with at least 22 additional patients developing a potentially fatal liver injury, according to **Kissei Pharmaceutical**, which distributes the medication in the country. On Friday, Kissei instructed doctors to stop prescribing the drug to new patients, marking a significant safety intervention in response to the mounting fatalities.

The deaths and serious adverse events represent a serious safety concern for the pharmaceutical product, which is used to treat rare immune system disorders. The exact timeline of these deaths and injuries was not specified in the available reporting, though the announcement came on May 15, 2026.

## Background on Tavneos and Its Use

Tavneos is a specialized medication developed by **Amgen** for treating rare immune-related diseases. The drug's distribution in Japan is handled by **Kissei Pharmaceutical**, a domestic pharmaceutical company responsible for bringing the medication to Japanese patients who need it.

The emergence of liver injuries as a serious side effect marks a critical safety signal. Liver damage can be life-threatening and may develop gradually or suddenly depending on the individual patient and circumstances. The fact that at least 22 patients developed potentially fatal liver injuries—in addition to the 20 confirmed deaths—suggests a pattern that warranted immediate medical intervention.

## What Prompted the Action

The decision by Kissei Pharmaceutical to halt new prescriptions came after the accumulation of adverse event reports. While the source does not provide specific details about how long these cases had been accumulating or what triggered the Friday announcement, the scale of the response indicates that medical authorities and the company determined the risk to new patients outweighed potential benefits.

This type of action—instructing healthcare providers to stop prescribing to new patients—typically occurs when a pharmaceutical company and regulators believe existing patients may need to continue treatment (due to lack of alternatives or critical need), but new patients should not be exposed to the identified risks. The instruction to doctors represents a measured but serious safety response.

## Implications for Patients and Healthcare

For patients currently taking Tavneos in Japan, the situation raises important questions about whether they should continue their medication, seek alternatives, or undergo additional monitoring. Kissei's guidance to halt new prescriptions does not necessarily mean existing patients should immediately stop taking the drug, but it does signal that the benefit-risk calculation has shifted significantly.

Healthcare providers in Japan will need to reassess treatment plans for patients on Tavneos and consider alternative therapies for rare immune diseases. This may be particularly challenging given that rare-disease medications often have limited alternatives, leaving some patients in difficult positions regarding their ongoing care.

## What's Next

The incident will likely trigger formal investigations by Japanese regulatory authorities to determine the full scope of the safety issue and whether similar problems have been reported in other countries where Tavneos is distributed. According to [WSJ](https://www.wsj.com/health/pharma/20-people-in-japan-died-after-taking-amgen-drug-40edaefd), Amgen will face scrutiny regarding whether adequate safety monitoring and warnings were in place before the deaths and injuries accumulated to this level.

Patients taking Tavneos should consult with their healthcare providers immediately to discuss their individual situations and whether continued use remains appropriate. Those experiencing symptoms such as jaundice, abdominal pain, or unusual fatigue should seek medical attention promptly, as these may indicate liver problems.

The case underscores the importance of ongoing pharmacovigilance—the monitoring of medications after they reach patients—and the critical role that healthcare providers and pharmaceutical companies play in identifying and responding to safety signals quickly.
