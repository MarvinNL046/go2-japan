Japan is poised to expand its defense industry globally as it eases decades-old restrictions on arms exports, capitalizing on surging international demand for weapons amid escalating geopolitical tensions. This shift, highlighted in a recent analysis by **CNBC**, comes as global military spending reached a record **$2.89 trillion** in 2025, marking the 11th consecutive year of increases according to the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI). The move could position Japanese firms, known for high-quality engineering, as key players in supplying allies with advanced systems like air defense missiles and naval vessels, though experts caution that inexperience and capacity issues may temper rapid growth.

## Background on Japan's Defense Export Shift

Japan's decision to relax arms export bans represents a significant departure from its post-World War II pacifist stance, which long prohibited the sale of lethal weapons abroad. These restrictions, in place for decades, were partially loosened in 2014 to allow exports of non-lethal equipment and joint development, but results were underwhelming. According to the International Institute of Strategic Studies, exports of finished products since 2014 have been limited to just three fixed deals, often attributed to Japan's lack of international marketing savvy and promotional experience in the defense sector.

The current easing builds on that foundation, driven by a volatile global security environment. Conflicts such as the ongoing Russia-Ukraine war and potential escalations involving Iran have heightened demand for affordable, reliable arms. Countries are seeking alternatives to traditional suppliers like the United States, where alliance commitments are under scrutiny and production backlogs are growing. Hirohito Ogi, a senior research fellow at the Tokyo-based Institute of Geoeconomics and former official in Japan's Ministry of Defense, told CNBC that nations are "desperate" for items like air defense missiles, artillery shells, and armored vehicles—areas where Japanese companies could gain traction.

South Korea provides a compelling model for Japan's ambitions. Korean defense firms have thrived in recent years by offering weapons that are cheaper, faster to produce, and of comparable quality to U.S. systems, especially as the Ukraine conflict prolongs demand. Japanese industry leaders, with their reputation for precision engineering, may follow suit, focusing initially on niches where they excel rather than broad market dominance.

## Opportunities in Key Export Areas

Japan's strengths lie in high-tech domains, particularly maritime and air defense systems, making these prime targets for early exports. Experts anticipate a heavy emphasis on advanced radar systems, patrol vessels, and co-produced interceptor missiles to enhance maritime domain awareness. Stephen Nagy, professor of politics and international studies at Tokyo's International Christian University, described Japanese engineering as "top-tier," with the next-generation Global Combat Air Programme fighter jet—developed jointly with Britain and Italy—as a "crown jewel."

This sixth-generation fighter is set to replace the Eurofighter Typhoon in the U.K. and Italy, as well as Japan's Mitsubishi F-2. While full-scale exports of such advanced aircraft may take years, the program underscores Japan's rising profile in collaborative defense projects.

Recent deals signal momentum. In April 2026, Australia inked contracts for its first three general-purpose frigates, to be constructed by **Mitsubishi Heavy Industries** based on the upgraded Japanese Mogami-class design. Interest is also brewing elsewhere: Indonesia has expressed desire for high-speed patrol boats, while the Philippines is in discussions for potential transfers of Japanese defense equipment. New Zealand has reportedly shown interest in the same Mogami-class frigates ordered by Australia.

These developments align with broader alliances in the Indo-Pacific, where nations like the Philippines and Australia are bolstering defenses against tensions in the South China Sea and Taiwan Strait. A recent Integrated Air and Missile Defense drill in the Philippines, involving Japanese Self-Defense Forces and U.S. troops, highlighted such cooperation, with Japanese personnel displaying national flags alongside missile systems.

## Challenges Facing Japan's Defense Ambitions

Despite the promise, hurdles abound. Japanese firms lack the industrial capacity and export experience to scale quickly, potentially cramping their efforts. Nagy noted that companies will likely target "specialized, high-tech niches among trusted allied partners" rather than competing in the wider global arms market immediately. Cost-competitiveness is another issue; while quality is unmatched, pricing must align with budget-conscious buyers.

Ogi pointed to the 2014 policy's lackluster outcomes as a cautionary tale, linking them to Japan's unfamiliarity with international promotion. Building a robust export pipeline will require investment in marketing, supply chains, and partnerships—steps that could take time amid domestic priorities like bolstering Japan's own Self-Defense Forces.

## What's Next for Japan's Defense Sector

Looking ahead, Japan's defense exports are expected to grow incrementally, starting with allied nations in Asia-Pacific and Europe. Success in deals like Australia's frigates could pave the way for more, especially as global spending trends upward. The Global Combat Air Programme, slated for deployment in the coming decade, may open doors to fighter jet sales, further elevating Japan's role.
