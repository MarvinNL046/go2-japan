New Zealand officials in Auckland rejected an application on April 29, 2026, to erect a statue commemorating the so-called "comfort women" enslaved by Japan during the Second World War, following objections from Tokyo that it could strain diplomatic relations. The Korean Garden Trust had proposed installing the monument at Barry’s Point reserve in the Takapuna suburb, but the local council declined after a public consultation process, citing community feedback including a letter from Japan's ambassador warning of potential division between Japanese and Korean communities.

### Background on the Comfort Women Issue

The "comfort women" refers to women who were forced or tricked into sexual slavery by the Japanese military from 1932 to 1945, primarily during Japan's imperial expansion in Asia leading up to and through the Second World War. Historians estimate that up to **200,000** women were affected, mostly from Korea, but also from China, south-east Asia, and smaller numbers from Japan and Europe. These women were euphemistically called "comfort women"—a term still used by Japan, though survivors have objected to it as minimizing their suffering.

According to testimony from survivors, the women were held in frontline military brothels and forced to have sex with **10 to 30** Japanese soldiers per day. Forced abortions were commonplace, and the conditions were brutal, reflecting a system of organized sexual exploitation tied to Japan's wartime efforts. The issue only gained widespread public attention in the early 1990s when the first survivor came forward in South Korea, straining relations between Japan and its neighbors ever since.

This historical atrocity remains a flashpoint in East Asian diplomacy. Japan has acknowledged aspects of the forced recruitment but has faced criticism for not fully accepting responsibility or providing adequate reparations. The term "comfort women" itself is contentious, as it originated from Japanese military documentation and is seen by many as sanitizing the reality of rape and enslavement.

### The Auckland Proposal and Japan's Response

The Korean Garden Trust aimed to honor the survivors with a statue similar to those erected elsewhere, viewing it as a way to educate the public and reflect on wartime history. The proposed site in Auckland's Barry’s Point reserve was intended to serve as a peaceful memorial, recognizing the significance of the women's stories.

However, the plan drew strong opposition from Japan. Japanese Ambassador to New Zealand **Makoto Osawa** submitted a letter during the consultation, arguing that the statue could "cause division and conflict within New Zealand’s wonderful multi-ethnic and multicultural society and between Japanese and Korean communities." New Zealand's Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade confirmed that the Japanese government had made "formal representations" about the proposal, emphasizing potential harm to bilateral ties.

Auckland council's board chair **Trish Deans** described the decision as "difficult" and not made lightly. The council reviewed staff advice and community submissions, many of which supported the statue as an educational tool. Deans acknowledged the historical importance, stating, "We recognise the significance of the history the statue represents, and we acknowledge the survivors whose stories it seeks to honour." Despite this, the weight of objections, including diplomatic concerns, led to the rejection.

### International Context of Comfort Women Memorials

Memorials to the comfort women have proliferated since the first "peace statue" was installed in Seoul in 2011, with dozens more appearing in cities worldwide. These monuments often depict a young woman in traditional attire, symbolizing resilience and remembrance. Japan has consistently protested their erection, viewing them as politically motivated and one-sided.

Notable past conflicts include the 2018 termination of Osaka's 60-year sister city relationship with San Francisco after the U.S. city approved a similar statue. In 2020, Japan reacted angrily to statues in South Korea that appeared to show former Prime Minister **Shinzo Abe** prostrating himself before a young woman, interpreting it as a direct insult. More recently, in 2025, a peace statue in Berlin was removed following a years-long dispute between Japan and Germany.

These incidents highlight Japan's ongoing efforts to counter what it sees as biased portrayals of its history. The Auckland rejection marks another instance where diplomatic pressure has succeeded in preventing such a memorial, though it has drawn criticism from activists who argue it silences victims' voices.
