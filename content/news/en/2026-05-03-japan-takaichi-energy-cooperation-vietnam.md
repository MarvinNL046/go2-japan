Japan's Prime Minister **Sanae Takaichi** met her Vietnamese counterpart, **Le Minh Hung**, in Hanoi on May 2, 2026, pledging deeper cooperation on energy and critical minerals to strengthen bilateral ties. The summit, focused on economic security amid regional tensions, resulted in the signing of six agreements covering technology, agriculture, space, and infrastructure, as both nations seek to diversify supply chains and address shared concerns over China's territorial claims in the East and South China Seas.

## Background on the Japan-Vietnam Summit

The meeting between **Sanae Takaichi** and **Le Minh Hung** underscores Japan's strategic push to bolster partnerships in Southeast Asia, particularly with Vietnam, one of its key economic allies. According to Al Jazeera, the two leaders identified economic security as a new priority for cooperation. This includes efforts to ensure stable supplies of critical minerals, which are essential for industries like electronics and renewable energy—sectors where Japan heavily invests.

The agreements signed during the Hanoi visit span a broad range of areas, from infrastructure development to agricultural exchanges and even space technology collaboration. **Takaichi** emphasized the need for close coordination to reinforce supply chains, a response to global disruptions in trade and resources. Meanwhile, **Hung** highlighted the reaffirmation of peaceful dispute resolution in the South China Sea based on international law, reflecting mutual wariness of escalating regional conflicts.

Japan and Vietnam's alignment stems from overlapping geopolitical interests. Both nations face assertive claims from China in overlapping maritime zones—the East China Sea for Japan and the South China Sea for Vietnam. This has prompted them to hedge against uncertainties, including potential trade disruptions driven by United States policies. Vietnam, as a manufacturing hub, benefits from Japanese investment, though recent data shows challenges: new Japanese investments fell about 75 percent year-on-year to **$233 million** in the first quarter of 2026, even as bilateral trade grew 12.3 percent to **$13.7 billion** over the same period, per Vietnamese government and customs figures.

This summit builds on longstanding ties, with Japan being one of Vietnam's largest foreign investors. The focus on energy cooperation is particularly timely, as both countries navigate the global shift toward sustainable resources and the need for reliable mineral supplies for batteries, semiconductors, and green technologies.

## Implications for Regional Energy Security

The pledge for deeper energy ties could reshape supply dynamics in Asia. Critical minerals, such as lithium, cobalt, and rare earths, are vital for Japan's high-tech economy and Vietnam's growing industrial base. By strengthening coordination, the two nations aim to mitigate risks from over-reliance on single suppliers, often linked to geopolitical hotspots.

For Japan, this cooperation supports its energy diversification strategy, reducing dependence on imports from volatile regions. Vietnam, with its emerging mining sector and strategic location, stands to gain from Japanese technology transfers in energy exploration and processing. The agreements on infrastructure could accelerate joint projects, like power plants or renewable energy initiatives, fostering mutual economic resilience.

However, the source notes a dip in investments, suggesting that while trade is robust, capital flows face hurdles—possibly due to global economic slowdowns or regional tensions. This context highlights the summit's role in signaling commitment to recovery and expansion, without speculating on immediate outcomes.
