# Anti-War Protests Erupt in Japan Over PM Takaichi's Defense Reforms

In Tokyo, thousands of protesters have taken to the streets in the largest anti-war demonstrations in decades, decrying Prime Minister **Sanae Takaichi**'s push to strengthen Japan's military posture. Since Takaichi assumed power in October 2025, her administration has moved to ease post-war pacifist restrictions, including lifting a ban on lethal weapons exports on 21 April, sparking widespread alarm over Japan's shift from its long-held renunciation of war. These events, unfolding amid heightened regional tensions with neighbors like China and North Korea, highlight a deepening divide over the nation's identity and security role.

## Background on Japan's Pacifist Legacy and Security Shifts

Japan's post-World War II constitution, enacted in 1947, embodies the country's commitment to peace, particularly through **Article 9**, which renounces war as a sovereign right and prohibits the maintenance of armed forces. This framework emerged just two years after the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki by the United States, which killed some 200,000 people by the end of the conflict. For over 75 years, this pacifist stance has defined Japan's national identity, fostering a society that prioritizes harmony and avoids military entanglement.

However, evolving geopolitical realities have prompted successive leaders to challenge these boundaries. Japan occupies a volatile neighborhood, bordered by an assertive China, an unpredictable North Korea, and Russia, while its key ally, the United States, urges greater contributions to regional security. Conservative politicians, especially from the ruling Liberal Democratic Party, have long advocated revising the constitution to formalize the role of the Self-Defense Forces.

A pivotal moment came under former Prime Minister **Shinzo Abe**, who in 2015 secured passage of a controversial security bill through the Diet. This legislation expanded the Self-Defense Forces' mandate to include limited collective self-defense, allowing Japan to support allies under attack. Takaichi, continuing this trajectory, argues that the postwar framework no longer aligns with contemporary threats. Her government's recent decision to lift the long-standing ban on exporting lethal weapons—framed as essential mutual support among allies in a severe security environment—has intensified debates. According to the [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c4g93v8jqnyo), this step marks a significant departure, enabling Japan to supply arms to partners facing aggression.

These changes build on broader trends, such as plans to deploy missiles on islands near Taiwan by 2031, reflecting Japan's strategic pivot. Yet, they evoke fears that the nation is inching toward becoming "war-capable," eroding the peace that Article 9 has safeguarded.

## The Rising Tide of Protests

Public demonstrations in Japan are typically subdued, reflecting cultural norms of social harmony and aversion to disruption. When large crowds mobilize, it signals profound unease. Current protests, swelling in Tokyo and beyond, draw from diverse generations, not just wartime survivors or elders clinging to the past.

In one vivid scene outside the prime minister's office, demonstrators gathered under clearing skies after rain, their chants amplifying as numbers grew. Placards bore stark messages like "No War" in bold kanji, while participants waved sodden flags. Younger voices, including those in their twenties and thirties, joined the fray, underscoring the issue's broad resonance.

Akari Maezono, a woman in her 30s, voiced frustration while holding peace lanterns: "I'm angry that these changes could be made without properly listening to us, the public." An older protester, standing with a red banner, emphasized the constitution's protective role: "Article 9 in particular must be protected at all costs. It kept Japan from being drawn into past conflicts like the US-Iran war. Without it, we surely would have entered the war by now."

These gatherings, the largest anti-war actions in decades, protest not only arms exports but the broader erosion of pacifism. They coincide with anniversaries like the 80th of the Hiroshima bombing, amplifying calls to preserve Japan's "no more war" ethos.

## What's Next

The momentum of these protests could pressure Takaichi's administration to reconsider its pace on defense reforms. Public opinion, historically pacifist, may sway parliamentary debates on constitutional amendments. Meanwhile, regional dynamics—such as North Korean missile tests or Chinese activities—will likely accelerate Japan's military buildup, regardless of domestic pushback.

Observers anticipate more rallies, especially around key dates like constitutional remembrance events. Takaichi's government maintains that enhanced defense is vital for deterrence, but balancing this with public sentiment will test her leadership. For Japan, the path forward hinges on reconciling its pacifist roots with a more assertive security posture, ensuring the "No War" cries do not fade into history.
