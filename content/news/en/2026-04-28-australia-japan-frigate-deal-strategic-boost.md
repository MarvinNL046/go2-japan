# Australia Buys Japanese Mogami Frigates in Record Defense Deal

Australia and Japan have sealed a significant defense agreement, with Canberra committing to acquire three upgraded **Mogami-class** frigates from Tokyo under **Project Sea 3000**. The deal, signed earlier this month by the two nations' defense ministers aboard the Japanese ship **JS Kumano** in Melbourne, represents Japan's biggest defense export to date and seeks to address the Royal Australian Navy's (RAN) shrinking surface combatant fleet, which is projected to reach its lowest numbers since World War II. This move not only bolsters Australia's naval capabilities but also strengthens strategic ties between the allies amid regional security challenges.

## Background on the Frigate Acquisition

The agreement comes as Australia works to revitalize its warship program amid ongoing concerns over fleet size and modernization. According to [Defense News](https://www.defensenews.com/global/asia-pacific/2026/04/27/massive-frigate-buy-from-japan-jolts-australian-warship-program/), the RAN's surface combatants have been dwindling, prompting urgent action through **Project Sea 3000**. The frigates in question are upgraded versions of Japan's **Mogami-class**, which were showcased during a visit to the Yokosuka naval base—home to the Japan Maritime Self-Defence Force (JMSDF)—in September 2022.

**Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (MHI)**, a key player in Japan's shipbuilding sector, will construct the initial three 4,800-ton vessels in Japan. The first frigate is scheduled for delivery by December 2029. Following this, an additional eight frigates will be built locally in Western Australia, fostering technology transfer and local industry growth. The total project is estimated to cost up to **A$20 billion (US$14.4 billion)** over the next decade—a figure that has doubled from estimates two years prior, reflecting expanded scope and inflation pressures.

This deal marks a milestone for Japan, which has been gradually easing its export restrictions on defense equipment. Previously focused on domestic needs due to post-war constitutional limits, Tokyo's shipbuilding industry now gains a major international boost. The **Mogami-class** frigates are noted for their advanced general-purpose design, incorporating stealth features, multi-mission capabilities, and integration with modern sensor and weapon systems. While specifics on upgrades for the Australian variants remain limited in available details, they are positioned as among the most sophisticated in their class.

Australian Defence Industry Minister **Pat Conroy** highlighted the urgency and innovation of the acquisition, stating: “This is the fastest acquisition for the Royal Australian Navy in peacetime. We’re working closely with Japanese and Australian industry partners as we acquire one of the most, if not the most, advanced general-purpose frigate in the world.” RAN Rear Admiral **Stephen Hughes**, Head of Naval Capability, echoed this enthusiasm, describing the frigates as “a game-changer from a capability perspective,” though further details on their integration were not fully elaborated in reports.

The signing ceremony aboard **JS Kumano** underscores the deepening military cooperation. Japan, long a partner in the U.S.-led alliances in the Asia-Pacific, sees this as an extension of trilateral frameworks like AUKUS, though the frigate deal stands independently. For context, the Yokosuka base, where early **Mogami-class** vessels operate, is a hub of JMSDF activities and a point of interest for those exploring Japan's naval heritage. Visitors to [Yokosuka](https://go2-japan.com/city/yokosuka/) can glimpse the base's operations, offering insights into Japan's evolving defense posture.

## Strategic Implications for Japan and Australia

This purchase jolts Australia's warship program by injecting new vessels into a fleet facing obsolescence. The RAN currently operates aging platforms, and without swift replenishment, its operational readiness could suffer. The **Mogami-class** acquisition addresses this by providing versatile frigates suitable for anti-submarine warfare, surface combat, and regional patrols—critical in the Indo-Pacific where tensions with China persist.

For Japan, the export success validates its push into global defense markets. MHI's involvement not only secures revenue but also positions Japanese technology as competitive against European and U.S. rivals. The deal advances bilateral strategic alignment, building on joint exercises and intelligence-sharing. Economically, it supports jobs in Japan's shipyards, particularly in Nagasaki and Yokohama, where MHI facilities are concentrated. This could indirectly enhance Japan's profile as a reliable partner, potentially opening doors for further collaborations.

However, challenges remain. Building in Australia requires seamless technology transfer, and the doubled budget signals potential overruns. Speculation on exact upgrade details—such as enhanced missile systems or Australian-specific integrations—is premature without official releases. The source notes the fleet's historical low, but exact current numbers are not specified, emphasizing the reversal's importance without quantifying the jolt's full scale.

## What's Next for the Program

The first frigate's 2029 delivery sets a timeline for RAN integration, with local builds in Western Australia starting soon after. Ongoing collaboration between MHI, Australian firms, and the JMSDF will focus on training and interoperability. Future expansions could include more exports or joint development, though nothing beyond the 11 frigates is confirmed.

Australia aims to sustain momentum, possibly accelerating other acquisitions. For Japan, this paves the way for additional deals, aligning with its 2022 defense export policy reforms. Monitoring budget adherence and delivery schedules will be key, as delays could affect fleet goals.

This landmark agreement not only revitalizes Australia's navy but cements Japan as a pivotal defense exporter, fostering enduring ties.
