A staffer at **Asahiyama Zoo** in the northern Japanese city of **Asahikawa** has allegedly confessed to police that he disposed of his wife's body in the zoo's incinerator, prompting authorities to delay the facility's seasonal reopening and launch a criminal investigation. The incident, reported last week, has cast a shadow over what was set to be a major tourist draw during Japan's Golden Week holiday starting Wednesday. According to local media and **BBC** reports, the zoo, which had been closed for maintenance since April 8, will now remain shut until at least Friday as police search the grounds.

## Background on the Incident

The case came to light when a friend of the missing woman reported her disappearance to authorities, leading to the employee's confession. Police conducted a search of the zoo premises last week after he admitted to using the incinerator—typically employed for disposing of animal carcasses—to handle his wife's body. Details on the circumstances of her death remain unclear, with no official confirmation yet on whether foul play is involved beyond the disposal method. Local reports indicate the employee works at the zoo, but his identity and specific role have not been disclosed publicly.

**Asahiyama Zoo**, established in 1967, is renowned across Japan for its innovative animal enclosures that bring visitors unusually close to wildlife. Features like glass domes and overhead cages for observing penguins and seals have made it a standout attraction, drawing more than one million visitors annually. Located in Hokkaido's Asahikawa, the zoo has become a symbol of the region's commitment to experiential tourism, especially during peak seasons like Golden Week, when families flock to northern Japan for cherry blossoms and outdoor activities. The facility's three-week maintenance closure was intended to prepare for this influx, but the shocking revelation has upended those plans.

Authorities have emphasized that the investigation is ongoing and focused solely on the incident, with no indication that zoo animals or visitors were endangered. The incinerator's use for animal remains suggests it was accessible to staff, but specifics on security protocols or how the act went undetected initially are not yet public. Mayor **Hirosuke Imazu** of Asahikawa addressed the situation in a press conference on Tuesday, describing it as an "unprecedented crisis" that no one could have predicted. He expressed personal anxiety over the magnitude of the event while assuring the public that preparations continue for a safe reopening.

The zoo's popularity has helped position Asahikawa as a must-visit stop for travelers exploring beyond Tokyo and Kyoto.
